Anticonvulsants


Pharmacokinetics

Anticonvulsants are absorbed by various routes, including oral, intravenous, and intramuscular administration. They are metabolized in the liver and excreted in the urine.

Pharmacodynamics

Anticonvulsants work by stabilizing neuronal membranes, decreasing neuronal excitability, and increasing the threshold for seizure activity. They may also act on neurotransmitters involved in seizure activity, such as gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA).

Drug Classifications

  • Barbiturates
  • Benzodiazepines
  • Hydantoins
  • Carbamazepine and related drugs
  • Valproic acid and related drugs

Routes of Administration

  • Oral
  • Intravenous
  • Intramuscular

Adverse Drug Reactions

  • Drowsiness and dizziness
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Ataxia and tremor
  • Rash and other dermatological reactions
  • Leukopenia and thrombocytopenia

Drug Interactions

  • Other anticonvulsants
  • Antidepressants
  • Antipsychotics
  • Oral contraceptives

Medication Errors

Medication errors involving anticonvulsants can have serious consequences, including seizures and other neurological complications. Common errors include incorrect dosing, incorrect administration, and failure to monitor for adverse effects or drug interactions.
