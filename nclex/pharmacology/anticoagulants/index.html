Anticoagulants


Pharmacokinetics

Anticoagulants are absorbed by various routes, including oral, intravenous, and subcutaneous administration. They are metabolized in the liver and excreted in the urine.

Pharmacodynamics

Anticoagulants prevent the formation of blood clots by interfering with the clotting cascade or platelet function. They do not dissolve existing clots but rather prevent the formation of new ones.

Drug Classifications

  • Direct thrombin inhibitors
  • Factor Xa inhibitors
  • Vitamin K antagonists
  • Heparin and low-molecular-weight heparin

Routes of Administration

  • Oral
  • Intravenous
  • Subcutaneous

Adverse Drug Reactions

  • Bleeding
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Drug-induced hepatitis
  • Hypersensitivity reactions

Drug Interactions

  • Aspirin and other nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
  • Antidepressants
  • Antifungal agents
  • Anticoagulant/antiplatelet agents

Medication Errors

Medication errors involving anticoagulants can have serious consequences, including bleeding and thromboembolism. Common errors include incorrect dosing, incorrect administration, and failure to monitor for adverse effects or drug interactions.
